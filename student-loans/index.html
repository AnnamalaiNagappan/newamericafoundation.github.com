The clock is ticking on federal student loans. As of July 1, 2013, interest rates on newly issued subsidized Stafford loans are scheduled to double from 3.4 to 6.8 percent. And a clear divide has emerged in the debate: Fixed rates versus market-based rates. The rates are currently set arbitrarily as fixed numbers, but a market-based rate could cost students substantially less than a fixed rate in the current economy.

Most analyses of the interest rate proposals have focused on subsidized Stafford loans, the ones on which rates are set to rise. However, several of the solutions on the table also address unsubsidized Stafford loans, which make up the majority of undergraduate loans distributed by the government and are available regardless of financial need. Lower rates on those loans could mean lower monthly payments for all borrowers next year.

House Republicans last month passed a bill to create variable interest rates – market-based rates that vary each year for the borrower until the loan is paid off – while most of the other proposals outlined set the interest rate over the entire life of the loan. Members of the Senate have released multiple proposals and voted on (but have not yet passed) several fixes. Below, we’ve outlined several of the most prominent proposals currently being debated:

Bipartisan Senate proposal

This proposal emerged as a last-minute solution just days before the scheduled increase in rates. Introduced by Sens. Manchin (D-WV), King (I-ME), Coburn (R-OK), Burr (R-NC), and Alexander (R-TN), it would tie rates to the rate on the 10-year Treasury note plus 1.85 percent for all undergraduate Stafford loans. Graduate Stafford loans would be set at the 10-year Treasury rate plus 3.4 percent. This calculator uses the undergraduate rate.

Obama's 2014 budget proposal

President Obama released an interest rate reform proposal in his fiscal year 2014 budget that institutes a market-based rate. The rate would be based on the rate on the 10-year Treasury note plus 0.93 percent for subsidized Stafford loans or plus 2.93 percent for unsubsidized Stafford loans.

Temporary extension of 3.4% rate

Currently, rates are set at 3.4 percent on subsidized Stafford loans and 6.8 percent on unsubsidized Stafford loans. Some Democratic senators have proposed extending the 3.4 percent rate for another two years, without altering rates on unsubsidized Stafford loans.

Rates double on subsidized Stafford loans

Current law says that on July 1, 2013, rates on subsidized Stafford loans reset to 6.8 percent, the same as the rate on unsubsidized Stafford loans. If Congress fails to act before the start of the 2013-14 school year, rates will remain at 6.8 percent for both loan types indefinitely.

*Stafford loans are the federal student loans provided to undergraduates. Subsidized Stafford loans are available primarily to low-income students and are limited to $3,500 in the first year of school, $4,500 in the second year, and $5,500 thereafter. Unsubsidized Stafford loans are available regardless of financial need and are limited to $9,500 in the first year, $10,500 in the second, and $12,500 thereafter. Total loans may not exceed $57,500, including the $23,000 maximum in subsidized Stafford loans. For dependent students, total loans may not exceed $31,000.
